\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:introduction}

\section{Motivation}
In Tunisia, the Baccalaureate examination is more than a school exam. It is a major academic and social milestone that strongly influences university access and future opportunities. Because of its importance, many students rely on private tutoring to prepare effectively. This has created a large parallel support system outside regular school, but it also places a financial burden on families and remains limited by time, availability, and class size. In group settings, some students also hesitate to ask basic questions, which can leave important gaps unresolved.

Large language models (LLMs) offer a possible way to make this kind of support more accessible. They can provide immediate explanations, personalized practice, and a space where students can ask questions freely. However, general-purpose systems are not well adapted to the Tunisian Baccalaureate context. They may use methods that are outside the official curriculum or present solutions in a style that does not match the expectations of Tunisian exam correction. In mathematics, this is especially important, since students are evaluated not only on correctness, but also on how they structure and write their reasoning.

This thesis is motivated by the question of whether an AI tutor can be adapted to this specific educational setting rather than simply relying on a general-purpose chatbot.

\section{Problem Statement}
The main challenge is to build a system that answers mathematics questions in a way that is both correct and useful for Tunisian Bac students. This requires staying within the official curriculum, avoiding methods that are not accepted in the exam, and generating answers in a form close to official corrections. A further difficulty is that the relevant source material is not readily available in machine-readable form. Much of it exists only as scanned pages, printed books, or handwritten corrections, which first need to be digitized and organized before they can be used in a retrieval system.

\section{Approach}
To address this problem, this thesis develops and evaluates an AI tutor for Tunisian Baccalaureate mathematics based on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). The system uses a curated corpus of past Bac exams, exercise series, and course material that was digitized into structured \LaTeX{}. These documents are indexed in a ChromaDB vector database using BGE-M3 embeddings and retrieved at runtime to support answer generation.

The thesis compares three approaches. The first is a RAG system that grounds answers in retrieved documents. The second is a prompt-only baseline that relies on the model's internal knowledge without retrieval. The third is a hybrid system that adapts its behavior according to retrieval confidence. Comparing these three systems makes it possible to test whether retrieval actually improves performance in this domain.

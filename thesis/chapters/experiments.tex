\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{subsec:evaluation-methodology}
\label{subsec:eval-setup}

Mathematical correctness, logical validity, and adherence to domain-specific
conventions cannot be captured by automated metrics like BLEU or
ROUGE~\cite{kasneci2023chatgpt_education}. We therefore designed a blind
human evaluation centered on expert judgment.

\subsubsection{Question Bank}

We used 20 questions across five categories (full list in
Appendix~\ref{app:questions}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Category~A} (5): Direct Bac-style exercises, likely to
    have near-exact corpus matches. Tests retrieval precision.

    \item \textbf{Category~B} (5): Novel exercises on Bac topics with
    different formulations. Tests generalization.

    \item \textbf{Category~C} (3): Informal student-style questions
    (incomplete phrasing, colloquial French). Tests coaching quality.

    \item \textbf{Category~D} (2): Tunisian Derja or mixed-language
    questions. Tests language robustness.

    \item \textbf{Category~E} (4): Out-of-scope methods (L'H\^opital,
    diagonalization, Taylor, Lebesgue). Tests guardrails. Binary pass/fail.
\end{itemize}

Categories A--D (15 questions) were graded on a four-criterion rubric.
Category~E was evaluated separately as a guardrail test.

\subsubsection{Grading Rubric}

Each graded question was scored per system on four criteria (0--5 scale,
equally weighted):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mathematical correctness}: validity of computations and
    reasoning.
    \item \textbf{Reasoning clarity}: logical structure and readability.
    \item \textbf{Pedagogical quality}: educational value beyond bare
    computation.
    \item \textbf{Bac style adherence}: resemblance to official Tunisian
    corrections.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Blind Protocol}
\label{subsec:blind-evaluation}

For each question, the three system outputs were randomly shuffled
(per-question seed) and presented under anonymous labels (Syst\`{e}me~X,
Y, Z). The evaluator---an experienced Tunisian Bac mathematics teacher---did
not know which label corresponded to which system. Answer sheets were
generated programmatically; the answer key was used to unmask identities
after grading (details in Appendix~\ref{app:rubric}).

\subsubsection{Example~1: Low Distance, Wrong Content (B02 --- Probabilités)}

\noindent\textbf{Query:} \textit{``Urne: 5 blanches, 3 noires. Tirage
successif sans remise de 3 boules. Calculer $P$(exactement 2 blanches).''}

This query achieved the lowest distance in the evaluation ($d = 0.40$),
yet the chapter match was a miss. The RAG system produced a brief answer
citing generic retrieved sources. The Prompt-Only system correctly
identified the exercise type and worked through both sub-questions with
explicit combinatorial reasoning. The Hybrid system referenced the context
for ``simultaneous'' draws---a different problem type than the sequential
draws the question specifies.

\noindent\textbf{Takeaway:} Even with the best retrieval distance, the
retrieved content addressed a variant of the problem, and the RAG
instruction to rely on context constrained the response.

\subsubsection{Example~2: Correct Chapter Match (D01 --- Suites réelles)}

\noindent\textbf{Query (Derja):} \textit{``Kifech nethbet elli suite
monotone et bornée convergente?''}

One of the few queries where retrieval found the correct chapter. All three
systems produced substantive answers. The quality gap was visibly narrower
than in Example~1, consistent with Category~D showing the smallest
inter-system gap in Table~\ref{tab:per-category}. This suggests that corpus
coverage, not embedding quality, is the binding constraint.

\subsubsection{Example~3: Subtopic Mismatch (C01 --- Continuité)}

\noindent\textbf{Query:} \textit{``Je comprends pas comment on étudie la
continuité d'une fonction en un point.''}

The student asks about \textit{pointwise} continuity. The retrieval found
material about continuity \textit{on intervals}---related but wrong
subtopic ($d = 0.92$, chapter match but wrong aspect). The RAG system
adapted the interval material but never provided the three conditions for
pointwise continuity. The Prompt-Only system stated the definition directly
and worked through a concrete example.

The Hybrid engine acknowledged the mismatch (``le contexte ne contient pas
la définition formelle'') but could not override its Case~A instructions.
This motivates threshold recalibration in future work.

\paragraph{Pattern.}
When retrieval finds the correct chapter (Example~2), the gap narrows.
When it returns semantically close but topically mismatched content
(Examples~1 and~3), it actively harms quality. Corpus coverage---not
embedding distance---is the primary bottleneck.

coverage, not embedding quality. Switching models would lower distances
but not fix the underlying corpus completeness issues.

\subsection{Digitization Quality}
\label{subsec:ocr-evaluation}

We evaluated Gemini's OCR output against manually corrected reference texts
on a stratified sample covering dense math notation, text-heavy proofs,
course material, and cleanly printed documents.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
CER (character-level Levenshtein distance, normalized by reference
length)~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}, WER (word-level), and \LaTeX{} math
expression accuracy (fraction of math expressions preserved exactly).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{OCR quality on five ground truth samples. Lower CER/WER is better;
higher \LaTeX{} accuracy is better.}
\label{tab:ocr-quality}
\begin{tabular}{llrrr}
\toprule
\textbf{Sample} & \textbf{Type} & \textbf{CER (\%)} & \textbf{WER (\%)} & \textbf{\LaTeX{} Acc.\ (\%)} \\
\midrule
Sample~1 & Correction (complexes)     & 5.4  & 10.0 & 66.7 \\
Sample~2 & Cours (suites)             & 4.2  & 5.5  & 99.0 \\
Sample~3 & Correction (intégrales)    & 0.0  & 0.0  & ---  \\
Sample~4 & Correction (probabilités)  & 4.1  & 5.8  & 100.0 \\
Sample~5 & Cours (théorèmes)          & 13.4 & 8.8  & 95.5 \\
\midrule
\textbf{Average} & & \textbf{5.4} & \textbf{6.0} & \textbf{97.1} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The average CER of 5.4\% and \LaTeX{} accuracy of 97.1\% indicate largely
faithful transcription. Four of five samples fall below 6\% CER, consistent
with published multimodal OCR benchmarks~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}. Sample~5
(dense theorem statements) was the weakest at 13.4\%, likely due to nested
math environments.

The corpus is not dominated by OCR noise, but the occasional errors
(roughly 1 in 20 characters, 1 in 30 math expressions) can corrupt
retrieved context. This affects only the RAG and Hybrid systems, giving
the Prompt-Only baseline an inherent advantage.

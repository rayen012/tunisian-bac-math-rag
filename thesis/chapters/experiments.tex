\subsection{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{subsec:evaluation-methodology}

Evaluating an AI tutoring system for mathematics is inherently challenging.
Unlike natural language generation tasks where automated metrics like BLEU
or ROUGE can serve as rough proxies, mathematical correctness requires
verifying logical validity, computational accuracy, and adherence to
domain-specific conventions that no automated metric captures
reliably~\cite{kasneci2023chatgpt_education}. We therefore designed a
blind human evaluation protocol centered on expert judgment from a
practicing Tunisian Baccalaureate mathematics teacher.

\subsubsection{Question Bank}

The evaluation used 20 questions distributed across five categories, each
designed to probe a different aspect of system behavior:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Category~A} (5 questions): Direct Bac-style exercises
    closely resembling real examination problems. These questions are
    likely to have near-exact matches in the corpus, testing whether the
    RAG system can retrieve and leverage relevant corrections.

    \item \textbf{Category~B} (5 questions): Novel chapter-based exercises
    on the same Bac topics but with different numbers, formulations, or
    problem structures. These test whether the systems can generalize
    beyond memorized examples.

    \item \textbf{Category~C} (3 questions): Informal student-style
    questions written as a real Tunisian student might type them—incomplete
    phrasing, colloquial French, conceptual rather than computational. These
    test pedagogical quality and coaching ability.

    \item \textbf{Category~D} (2 questions): Questions in Tunisian Arabic
    (Derja) or mixed French-Derja, testing language robustness and the
    system's ability to handle code-switching.

    \item \textbf{Category~E} (4 questions): Out-of-scope questions
    requesting methods explicitly outside the Tunisian Bac program
    (L'Hôpital's rule, matrix diagonalization, Taylor series, Lebesgue
    integration). These test the curriculum guardrail mechanism. Unlike
    categories A--D, these are evaluated on a binary pass/fail basis rather
    than a graded rubric.
\end{itemize}

Categories A through D (15 questions) were evaluated using a four-criterion
grading rubric. Category~E (4 questions) was evaluated separately as a
guardrail test.

\subsubsection{Grading Rubric}

Each of the 15 graded questions was scored independently for each system
on four criteria, each on a 0--5 scale:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mathematical correctness} (0--5): Are the computations and
    reasoning steps mathematically valid? A score of 0 indicates entirely
    incorrect or absurd content; 5 indicates a fully correct solution.

    \item \textbf{Reasoning clarity} (0--5): Is the reasoning clear, logical,
    and well-structured? Are the steps easy to follow?

    \item \textbf{Pedagogical quality} (0--5): Does the answer help the
    student understand the method, not merely obtain the result? Does it
    have educational value beyond a bare computation?

    \item \textbf{Bac style adherence} (0--5): Does the answer resemble an
    official Tunisian Baccalaureate correction? Does it use the formal
    conventions (``On a\ldots'', ``Donc\ldots'', ``D'après\ldots''),
    academic French, and the expected level of detail?
\end{enumerate}

The four criteria are equally weighted. No composite score was imposed
during grading; the teacher scored each criterion independently.

\subsubsection{Blind Evaluation Protocol}

To prevent evaluator bias, we implemented a fully blind grading procedure.
For each question, the three system outputs (RAG, Prompt-Only, Hybrid) were
randomly shuffled and presented under anonymous labels (Système~X, Système~Y,
Système~Z). The shuffling was performed per question using a fixed random
seed, so the mapping between anonymous labels and real system names varied
from one question to the next. The evaluator did not know which label
corresponded to which system at any point during grading.

The anonymous answer sheets and empty grading templates were generated
programmatically from the evaluation results and provided to the evaluator
as printable documents. After grading was complete, the answer key was used
to unmask the system identities and compute per-system statistics.

\subsubsection{Evaluator}

The evaluation was conducted by a single experienced Tunisian Baccalaureate
mathematics teacher. This evaluator was selected for domain expertise: deep
familiarity with the Bac correction conventions, the official curriculum
boundaries, and the pedagogical expectations of the Section Mathématiques
program. The limitation of relying on a single evaluator is acknowledged in
Section~\ref{subsec:limitations}.

\subsection{Digitization Quality Evaluation}
\label{subsec:ocr-evaluation}

The quality of the RAG system's knowledge base depends directly on the
accuracy of the digitization pipeline described in
Section~\ref{subsec:digitization}. Errors introduced during OCR—a misread
exponent, a dropped minus sign, a garbled fraction—propagate silently into
the vector store and can corrupt the retrieved context presented to the
generative model. To quantify this risk, we conducted a ground truth
evaluation of the Gemini multimodal OCR output against manually corrected
reference texts.

\subsubsection{Metric Selection}

We report three metrics, each capturing a different dimension of
digitization fidelity.

\paragraph{Character Error Rate (CER).}
CER is the standard metric for OCR evaluation~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}. It
is defined as the character-level Levenshtein edit distance between the OCR
output and the reference text, normalized by the length of the reference:
\[
\text{CER} = \frac{\text{editDistance}(\text{OCR}, \text{ref})}{|\text{ref}|}
\]
A CER of 0\% indicates a perfect transcription; a CER of 5\% means that
roughly one in twenty characters requires correction. CER captures all
errors equally—whitespace, punctuation, letters, and mathematical
symbols—without distinguishing their semantic impact.

We chose CER because it is the most widely used metric in the OCR
literature and allows comparison with published
benchmarks~\cite{wang2023ocrbench, mahdavi2019icdar_math}. Its main
limitation is that it treats all character errors equally: a misplaced
space and a flipped sign in an exponent count the same, even though the
latter is mathematically catastrophic.

\paragraph{Word Error Rate (WER).}
WER operates at the word level, computing the Levenshtein distance between
the tokenized OCR output and the tokenized reference, normalized by the
reference word count. WER penalizes word-level substitutions, insertions,
and deletions, and is less sensitive to minor formatting differences
(extra spaces, line break variations) than CER. It is commonly used in
speech recognition evaluation and has been adopted for OCR evaluation of
structured documents.

\paragraph{\LaTeX{} Math Expression Accuracy.}
Neither CER nor WER distinguishes mathematical content from natural
language text. A document could have perfect CER on the French prose but
corrupt every equation, or vice versa. To address this, we compute a
\LaTeX{}-specific metric: the fraction of math expressions in the
reference text (delimited by \texttt{\$...\$}, \texttt{\$\$...\$\$},
\texttt{\textbackslash(...\textbackslash)}, or
\texttt{\textbackslash[...\textbackslash]}) that appear exactly in the OCR
output. This metric directly measures what matters most for a mathematical
RAG system: whether the equations that will be embedded and retrieved are
faithful to the source material.

This metric is an implementation-specific design. We are not aware of a
published OCR metric that specifically targets \LaTeX{} math expression
fidelity, though related work on mathematical expression
recognition~\cite{mahdavi2019icdar_math} evaluates similar properties at
the symbol and structure level.


\subsubsection{Ground Truth Methodology}

We selected a stratified sample of digitized documents from the corpus,
aiming to cover the range of document types and difficulty levels present
in the knowledge base:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Corrections with dense mathematical notation (complex numbers,
    integrals)
    \item Corrections with text-heavy reasoning (proofs, sequence
    convergence)
    \item Course material with theorem statements and definitions
    \item Documents with fractions, summation notation, and multi-line
    equations (high OCR difficulty)
    \item Cleanly printed documents (expected low error rate, serving as
    a baseline)
\end{itemize}

For each selected document, the Gemini OCR output (\texttt{.tex} file
from GCS) was paired with a manually corrected reference version. The
manual correction was performed by the author, consulting the original
scanned image to resolve any ambiguities. The corrected reference preserves
the same \LaTeX{} formatting conventions as the OCR output so that the
comparison measures transcription accuracy rather than stylistic
preferences.

\subsubsection{Observations}

The average CER of 5.4\% and WER of 6.0\% indicate that the Gemini
multimodal OCR pipeline produces largely faithful transcriptions of the
source material. Four of the five samples fall below 6\% on both metrics,
which is consistent with the performance ranges reported for multimodal LLM
OCR on structured documents~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}. The near-perfect
scores on Sample~3 (CER and WER both 0.0\%) suggest that cleanly printed
correction pages with standard formatting pose little difficulty for the
model.

Sample~5 (cours, théorèmes) was the weakest performer at 13.4\% CER, which
is notably higher than the other samples. This document contained dense
theorem statements with nested mathematical environments and multi-line
display equations. The higher error rate likely reflects the difficulty of
parsing complex \LaTeX{} structures from scanned images, particularly when
line breaks, alignment environments, and nested delimiters are involved.

The \LaTeX{} math expression accuracy averaged 97.1\% across the four
samples where it could be measured, meaning that the vast majority of
mathematical expressions were transcribed without alteration. Sample~1
(complexes correction) was the exception, with only 2 of 3 math expressions
preserved exactly (66.7\%). While this sample size is too small to draw
strong conclusions from, it suggests that isolated errors in mathematical
notation do occur and can affect the correctness of retrieved context in the
RAG pipeline.

These results have two implications for the system evaluation. First, the
overall digitization quality is high enough that the knowledge base can be
considered a reasonably faithful representation of the source material—the
corpus is not dominated by OCR noise. Second, the occasional errors that do
occur (roughly 1 in 20 characters, and 1 in 30 math expressions) could
contribute to the lower performance of the RAG and Hybrid systems observed
in the blind evaluation (Section~\ref{subsec:results}): when the retrieved
context contains a corrupted equation or a garbled reasoning step, the
generative model must work around it, which may degrade answer quality. The
Prompt-Only system, which never accesses the corpus, is entirely immune to
this source of error.

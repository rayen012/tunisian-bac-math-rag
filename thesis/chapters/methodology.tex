\chapter{Methods}
\label{chap:Methods}

This chapter presents the three system variants used in the thesis: the RAG
system (Section~\ref{sec:rag-system}), the prompt-only baseline
(Section~\ref{sec:prompt-only}), and the hybrid system
(Section~\ref{sec:hybrid}). Since all three variants share the same overall
infrastructure, the chapter begins with the common pipeline before describing
the differences between them.

\section{System Overview}
\label{sec:system-overview}

We developed three system variants that share the same generative model,
persona, and output format, but differ in how they construct the context
given to the model. Two of the three variants (RAG and Hybrid) use
retrieval-augmented generation~\cite{lewis2020rag,gao2024rag_survey},
whereas the third (Prompt-Only) relies entirely on prompt engineering
without retrieval. The shared infrastructure is organized as a five-stage
pipeline tailored to the Tunisian Bac domain. The main differences between
the variants appear in the retrieval and generation stages.
Figure~\ref{fig:system-architecture} shows the complete pipeline.

The corpus is built from three types of material: past Bac exams with
corrections, chapter-based exercise series, and course documents. These
sources are first digitized into \LaTeX{} using Gemini as a multimodal OCR
tool. The digitized files are then normalized, split into chunks, embedded
with BGE-M3~\cite{chen2024bge_m3}, and stored in a local ChromaDB
instance~\cite{chromadb2024}. The final indexed collection contains 624
source files grouped into 19 chapters or categories and represented by
1{,}422 chunks.

At query time, the retrieval step starts by searching correction-style
documents, since these are the most useful when the goal is to reproduce the
form and phrasing of Bac solutions. A second pass for course material exists
as a fallback when correction retrieval is weak. In addition, when a
correction chunk is selected, the system retrieves its matching exercise
statement so that the model receives both the question and the correction
context together. The retrieved material is then inserted into a structured
prompt and sent to Gemini through Vertex AI for answer generation.

All three system variants share the same base model, the same persona
(``Monsieur Tounsi''), and the same general answer format. The main
difference between them is how they obtain domain knowledge at inference
time. The RAG system uses retrieved documents, the prompt-only baseline
relies on prompt-encoded knowledge without retrieval, and the hybrid system
adapts its behavior according to retrieval confidence. This setup makes the
comparison between the three approaches more controlled and easier to
interpret.

\section{RAG System}
\label{sec:rag-system}

This section presents the RAG system, which is the main technical
contribution of this thesis. The pipeline has six components: data
acquisition, digitization, database construction, embedding model selection,
two-stage retrieval, and answer generation.

\subsection{Data Acquisition}
\label{sec:data-acquisition}

The foundation of any RAG system is the quality of its knowledge
base~\cite{lewis2020rag, gao2024rag_survey}. Research on data quality in
AI systems has shown that downstream performance often depends more on data
quality than on model architecture, and that problems introduced early in
the data pipeline can propagate throughout the whole
system~\cite{sambasivan2021everyone}. This observation guided our approach:
we prioritized corpus quality and structural consistency over size.
Before this work, no publicly available, machine-readable corpus of Tunisian
Bac mathematics materials was available. The main sources were printed
examination books, handwritten corrections, and scanned course pages
prepared by experienced teachers.

\textbf{Physical data collection.} The raw material was collected during a
trip to Tunisia, where books and course materials were obtained from Bac
mathematics teachers. For printed material, a standard scanner or phone
camera provided sufficient quality. For handwritten corrections with dense
notation or faded ink, the corrections were manually rewritten on a tablet
(iPad with Apple Pencil) before scanning. This was time-consuming, but
necessary to improve data quality~\cite{sambasivan2021everyone,
wang2023ocrbench, mahdavi2019icdar_math}. Each exercise was scanned 
\textit{together with its correction} as a single
image or PDF, rather than separating the statement from the solution. Course
material was scanned as continuous documents for each chapter.

\textbf{Corpus structure.} The material was uploaded to Google Cloud
Storage~\cite{gcloud_storage2024} in a hierarchical structure:

\begin{verbatim}
BacMath_Raw_Data/
  01_Continuite_et_limites/
    Cours/                      -> course material
    series_et_corrections/      -> exercises + corrections
  ...
  20_Coniques/
  Bac_avec_corrections_Images/  -> past exam corrections
\end{verbatim}

This naming convention is machine-readable: the indexing pipeline extracts
document type, chapter, year, session, and exercise number directly from
folder paths using regular expressions. The corpus comprises 624 source
files spanning 19 chapters: 312 corrected exam pages, 250 series exercise
files, and 62 course material files, yielding 1{,}422 chunks after indexing.

\textbf{Paired exercise/correction acquisition.} Scanning each exercise
together with its correction was a deliberate design choice. In mathematics,
a correction is a complete reasoning chain whose structure depends on the
exercise statement. Separating the two would break this link. The retrieval
system also benefits from keeping them connected, since the most useful
retrieval target is a complete worked example rather than an isolated
solution. This is in line with prior work showing that structure-aware
document processing can improve retrieval
quality~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking, chen2024mog}.

\subsection{Mathematical Document Digitization}
\label{sec:digitization}

The next step converts scanned images into machine-readable \LaTeX{}.
Mathematical documents contain two-dimensional structures such as fractions,
subscripts, and integrals that standard OCR systems do not handle
well~\cite{wang2023ocrbench, mahdavi2019icdar_math}.

\textbf{Tool selection.} We initially considered
Mathpix~\cite{mathpix2024}, a commercial math OCR service, but its per-page
pricing made it impractical for a thesis prototype. Instead, we used Gemini
through Vertex AI. Benchmarking by Wang et al.~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}
reported strong OCR performance for Gemini among the multimodal models they
tested. For our material, which was mainly printed or cleanly rewritten,
Gemini provided sufficient quality at low cost. The decision was therefore
mainly practical and budget-related, rather than based on a claim that
Gemini is universally more accurate than Mathpix.

\textbf{Pipeline (\texttt{digitize.py}).} The digitization pipeline first
enumerates images and PDFs stored in Google Cloud Storage, then checks
whether a corresponding \texttt{.tex} file already exists so that previously
processed files can be skipped. Each remaining file is sent to Gemini with a
structured prompt requesting a faithful transcription into \LaTeX{}. The
result is then uploaded back to cloud storage. To make the process more
robust, the pipeline also includes retry logic with exponential backoff for
temporary API errors.

\textbf{Quality.} Digitization quality matters because errors can propagate
to later stages of the system. A misread formula may be embedded
incorrectly, retrieved later, and then passed to the language model as part
of the prompt. The ground-truth evaluation discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:ocr-evaluation} reported an average CER of 5.4\% and a
\LaTeX{} math expression accuracy of 97.1\%. This was sufficient for the
present corpus, but it does not mean the digitization was error-free.

\subsection{Database Construction and Indexing}
\label{subsec:database-construction}

\textbf{Migration to ChromaDB.} An early prototype used a JSON-based vector
store. It was sufficient for small initial experiments, but it became too
limited as the corpus grew because it required loading everything into
memory, did not support incremental updates, and offered no metadata
filtering. For this reason, the system was migrated to
ChromaDB~\cite{chromadb2024}, which provides persistent local storage,
metadata-based filtering, and stable upsert operations.

\textbf{Text normalization.} Before chunking, each \texttt{.tex} file is
cleaned with a light normalization step. Line endings are standardized,
whitespace is collapsed, and excessive newlines are reduced. The goal is not
to heavily rewrite the text, but simply to make the corpus more consistent
before indexing.

\textbf{Adaptive chunking.} The chunking strategy depends on document type.
Corrections are split into 1{,}500-character chunks with 200-character
overlap so that individual exercise solutions remain as intact as possible.
Course material uses larger 3{,}000-character chunks with 250-character
overlap, since theorem statements and explanations often need more context
to remain meaningful. Whenever possible, the splitter prefers natural break
points such as paragraph breaks, line breaks, or sentence endings instead of
cutting at arbitrary character positions. This follows earlier findings that
structure-aware chunking can improve retrieval quality~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking, chen2024mog}.

\textbf{Metadata extraction.} Each chunk is stored together with metadata
derived automatically from the Google Cloud Storage path. This includes the
document type (\texttt{bac\_officiel}, \texttt{serie}, or \texttt{cours}),
the chapter, year, session, exercise identifier, whether the chunk belongs
to a solution, a group identifier for companion pairing, the filename, and
the source URI. This metadata plays an important role in retrieval because
it allows filtering and exercise--correction pairing without manual
annotation.

\textbf{Incremental indexing.} To avoid rebuilding the full database after
every change, the indexing pipeline maintains a manifest file
(\texttt{index\_manifest.json}) containing each file's GCS generation number
and content hash. Only files whose content has changed are embedded again.
In practice, this reduced re-indexing time from minutes to seconds during
development.

\subsection{Embedding Model Selection}
\label{subsec:embedding-selection}

Retrieval quality depends strongly on the embedding model. If two queries
with different mathematical meaning are mapped to very similar vectors, the
retrieval step may return the wrong context, and later stages cannot fully
correct that error. This issue is particularly important in mathematics,
where even small notation changes such as $v(n)$ versus $u(n)$ can refer to
different objects.

\textbf{Initial experience.} Our first implementation used a Google
embedding model through Vertex AI. During testing, it did not always
separate closely related mathematical expressions clearly. In some cases, a
query about $v(n)$ retrieved chunks about $u(n)$. This is not surprising,
since general-purpose embedding models often treat small lexical differences
as minor, even though they can be mathematically important~\cite{mansouri2019tangent, pfahler2020math_embeddings}.
This observation came from development and testing rather than from a formal
controlled experiment.

\textbf{Migration to BGE-M3.} We then switched to
BGE-M3~\cite{chen2024bge_m3}, which is based on XLM-RoBERTa and supports up
to 8{,}192 tokens. Its multilingual coverage, relatively long context
window, and training design made it a better fit for our corpus, which mixes
French mathematical text, some Arabic content, and longer exercise-based
documents. After this change, the earlier confusion between $v(n)$ and
$u(n)$ was no longer observed in our tests.

In the implementation, the embedding model is wrapped as a custom ChromaDB
\texttt{EmbeddingFunction}. It is loaded once as a singleton, with automatic
GPU detection when available.

\subsection{RAG Retrieval and Generation Engine}
\label{subsec:retrieval-engine}

The runtime pipeline is implemented in \texttt{rag\_engine.py}. It is
independent of the user interface: the same \texttt{query(question, mode)}
method returns a structured result and can be called from Streamlit,
Jupyter, or the command line.

\textbf{Two-stage retrieval.} The first retrieval pass searches correction
documents (\texttt{is\_solution=true}) and returns up to $k_1 = 10$
candidates. The idea is pedagogical as much as technical: a corrected
exercise provides not only mathematical content, but also an example of the
style and phrasing expected in Bac solutions.

If the best result from this first pass has an L2 distance above 1.2, the
system launches a second pass over course material and retrieves up to
$k_2 = 8$ additional documents. This second pass is intended to provide
theorem-level support when no strong correction-style match is available. If
retrieval remains weak, the engine can fall back to a broader unfiltered
search.

\textbf{Similarity thresholds.} Two distance thresholds guide the retrieval
logic. A best distance of at most 1.2 is treated as a strong match
(Case~A). Results above 1.6 are considered too weak and are discarded. The
interval between these two values corresponds to weaker matches that may
still be usable in fallback situations. These thresholds were set
empirically for BGE-M3 on the present corpus.

\textbf{Context compilation.} The selected documents are converted into
structured context blocks that include metadata and explicit role markers
such as \texttt{[\'ENONC\'E]} and \texttt{[CORRECTION]}. To keep prompts
within a manageable size, each document is truncated to at most 2{,}000
characters and the total retrieved context is capped at 14{,}000
characters.

\textbf{Prompt and generation.} The retrieved context is inserted into a
system prompt built around the ``Monsieur Tounsi'' persona. This prompt also
contains syllabus constraints, anti-hallucination instructions,
anti-injection rules, and guidance on how to use the retrieved material. In
the strong retrieval case, the model is encouraged to stay close to the
style of the retrieved correction. In weaker cases, it is instructed to rely
more on theorem-based reasoning from the available context. Generation is
performed with Gemini through Vertex AI using a low temperature
($T = 0.15$) and a maximum of 4{,}096 output tokens. Retry logic with
exponential backoff is used for temporary API failures.

\textbf{Confidence and observability.} Each query returns not only the final
answer, but also diagnostic information such as timing, retrieval metadata,
and a confidence label. The confidence level is marked as \textit{fort},
\textit{moyen}, or \textit{faible} depending on the retrieval result and the
amount of useful context available. This made it easier to inspect system
behavior during testing and later analysis.

\subsection{Paired Retrieval of Exercise and Correction}
\label{subsec:paired-retrieval}

During testing, we observed that the system could retrieve a correction
without the corresponding exercise statement, or the opposite. This was a
problem because a mathematical correction is usually not self-contained: its
notation, assumptions, and goals depend directly on the exercise.

To address this, the engine performs an additional companion lookup after
retrieval. For each selected correction, it uses metadata such as
\texttt{chapter}, \texttt{type}, \texttt{year}, and \texttt{exo\_id} to
search for the matching exercise statement with
\texttt{is\_solution=false}. This step relies on direct metadata filtering
in ChromaDB rather than vector similarity search. The retrieved exercise
chunks are then added to the prompt before the correction, with an
\texttt{[\'ENONC\'E]} label.

This mechanism is similar in spirit to parent-document
retrieval~\cite{gao2024rag_survey}, but adapted to the specific structure of
mathematics exercises and corrections. In practice, it improved the
completeness of the context given to the model.

\subsection{Connection to the Tunisian Bac Context}
\label{subsec:bac-context}

The system design was shaped by several characteristics of the Tunisian Bac
mathematics setting. First, the curriculum is relatively fixed, so the tutor
must stay within the accepted program and avoid methods that are not
expected in the exam. Second, official corrections follow a recognizable
writing style, which motivated the retrieval of solved exercises as style
examples. Third, exercises and corrections naturally belong together, which
led to the companion retrieval mechanism described above. Finally, the
setting is partly bilingual: the source material is mainly in French with
mathematical notation in \LaTeX{}, while student questions may also contain
Tunisian Derja. This made multilingual retrieval support important for the
system.

\section{Prompt-Only Baseline}
\label{sec:prompt-only}

\subsection{Role and Rationale}

The prompt-only baseline is designed as a control condition without
retrieval. It receives the same student question as the RAG system and uses
the same Gemini model, temperature, and output budget, but it does not query
the vector database. Instead, the relevant domain constraints are provided
directly in the system prompt.

This baseline is important for the evaluation because it helps separate the
effect of retrieval from the effect of the language model itself. Recent
LLMs already contain strong parametric knowledge in standard mathematical
domains~\cite{brown2020gpt3,team2023gemini}, and well-structured prompts can
often guide that knowledge effectively~\cite{wei2022chain_of_thought}. For
that reason, the comparison is not simply between a "simple" and a
"complex" system. It is a test of whether retrieval adds value beyond what
the model can already produce when it is carefully instructed.

To keep the comparison fair, the prompt-only baseline shares the same
general setup as the RAG system. Both use the same persona, the same
syllabus constraints, the same answer format, and the same external API.
The main difference is that the prompt-only system relies entirely on
prompt-encoded guidance instead of retrieved documents.

\subsubsection{Prompt Architecture}

The system prompt was built gradually during development and serves several
functions at once. Its role is not only to make the model answer the
question, but to make it answer in a way that is useful for Tunisian Bac
students. In particular, it defines the persona of ``Monsieur Tounsi'', sets
the curriculum boundaries, specifies the expected answer structure, and adds
rules for uncertainty, refusal, and out-of-scope methods.

The prompt can be understood as having ten main components, following the
general idea of prompt patterns~\cite{white2023prompt_patterns}.

\begin{description}
    \item[Persona.] ``Monsieur Tounsi'' is presented as an experienced
    Tunisian mathematics teacher preparing students for the Baccalaureate.
    He explains mathematics in formal French, but uses short phrases in
    Tunisian Derja for encouragement and motivation. This Derja--French
    structure, referred to in the project as the ``Derja sandwich,'' was
    intended to reflect a familiar tutoring style while keeping the
    mathematical explanation formal.

    \item[Curriculum inventory.] The prompt includes the main Bac chapters
    and their allowed topics so that the model stays within the official
    syllabus even without retrieval.

    \item[Reasoning guardrails.] A six-step internal checklist guides the
    answering process: identify the topic, recall the relevant result,
    verify that the method is allowed, plan the solution, solve it, and
    check the final answer~\cite{wei2022chain_of_thought}.

    \item[Output structure.] The answer is organized into a fixed pedagogical
    format, including identification of the topic, recall of the relevant
    course material, method, application, conclusion, and verification.

    \item[Hallucination control.] Since the prompt-only system has no
    retrieved documents, the prompt explicitly tells the model not to invent
    exercise numbers, page references, or unsupported details.

    \item[Syllabus guard.] The prompt lists methods that are outside the Bac
    program, such as L'H\^opital's rule, Taylor series, and diagonalization,
    and instructs the model to avoid them.

    \item[Derja sandwich.] Each answer begins and ends with a short
    motivational sentence in Tunisian Derja, while the mathematical body
    remains in formal French.

    \item[Self-verification.] The model is instructed to include a visible
    verification step at the end of the answer.

    \item[Refusal policy.] The prompt defines when the system should refuse,
    especially for out-of-scope requests.

    \item[Confidence calibration.] A simple confidence label (high, medium,
    or low) is added based on the form of the question, since no retrieval
    score is available in this setting.
\end{description}

This prompt architecture did not come from a single design decision at the
start of the project. It was refined iteratively as recurrent failure modes
appeared during testing, especially curriculum violations, hallucinated
references, and unstable formatting.

\subsection{Confidence Without Retrieval}

Because this system does not retrieve documents, it cannot use retrieval
distance as a confidence signal. Instead, it assigns a simple heuristic
confidence label based on the form of the question. Questions that clearly
match common Bac topics are marked with higher confidence, while short or
ambiguous questions receive lower confidence. This mechanism is only a
practical approximation and should not be interpreted as a measure of model reliability.

\section{Hybrid Engine}
\label{sec:hybrid}

\subsection{Motivation}

The RAG and prompt-only systems represent two opposite strategies. The RAG
system always tries to use retrieved documents, while the prompt-only system
never uses retrieval at all. In practice, neither is always better.
Recent work on adaptive retrieval has shown that the decision of whether to
rely on retrieval can matter as much as retrieval itself~\cite{yan2024practical_rag,asai2023self_rag}.

Rather than introducing a separate learned system, it uses a signal that is
already available in the system: the L2 distance of the best retrieved
document.

\subsection{Routing Logic}

The hybrid engine starts with the same retrieval process as the RAG system.
It then chooses one of three generation strategies based on the best
retrieval distance, excluding companion documents.

When the best distance is at most 1.2, the query is treated as a strong
retrieval case (Case~A). In this situation, the system follows the RAG
strategy and relies mainly on the retrieved context. 

When the best distance falls between 1.2 and 1.6, the query is treated as a
partial retrieval case (Case~B). Here, the prompt includes both the
retrieved material and the broader curriculum guidance used in the
prompt-only system. The goal is to let the model benefit from retrieval
without forcing it to depend entirely on context that may be only partly
relevant.

If the best distance is above 1.6, the system treats retrieval as too weak
to be useful (Case~C). In that case, the engine falls back to the prompt-only strategy.

This routing logic can be seen as a simple approximation of adaptive
retrieval methods such as CRAG~\cite{yan2024practical_rag}, but with fixed
thresholds instead of a learned quality estimator.

\subsection{Thresholds and Confidence}

The two thresholds, 1.2 and 1.6, were chosen empirically during
development by inspecting retrieval behavior on test queries. They should
therefore be understood as corpus-specific settings rather than as generally
valid values.

Confidence is also tied to the routing case. In Case~A, the system uses the
same confidence logic as the RAG engine. In Case~B, confidence is limited to
a medium level because the answer is only partially grounded in retrieved
documents. In Case~C, the system falls back to the simpler heuristic used by
the prompt-only baseline.

\subsection{Relationship to Adaptive Retrieval}

Compared with more advanced adaptive retrieval systems, the hybrid engine is
deliberately simple. Its role in this thesis is not
to propose a new adaptive retrieval method in general, but to test whether a
lightweight form of retrieval-aware routing can improve robustness in a
small, specialized educational corpus.

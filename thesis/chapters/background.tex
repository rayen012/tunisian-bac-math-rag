\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

This chapter goes over the main ideas behind the system we built. We cover language models, embeddings, retrieval-augmented generation, prompt engineering, hybrid architectures, vector databases, OCR for math, and AI in education.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Large Language Models}
\label{subsec:llm-background}

\subsubsection{Transformer Architecture}

Most modern language models rely on the Transformer, proposed by Vaswani et al.~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. The key idea is self-attention: instead of reading words one by one like older recurrent networks, a Transformer looks at all words at once and figures out which ones are related. The attention formula is:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
\label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}

Here $Q$, $K$, $V$ are the query, key, and value matrices, and $d_k$ is the key dimension. Multi-head attention repeats this with different learned projections so the model can pick up on different kinds of relationships in the same sentence.

\subsubsection{Encoder vs.\ Decoder Models}

There are two main flavours of Transformer models. Encoder-only models like BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert} are trained by masking words and predicting them, which makes them good at understanding text and computing embeddings. Decoder-only models like GPT-3~\cite{brown2020gpt3}, LLaMA~\cite{touvron2023llama}, and Gemini~\cite{team2023gemini} are trained to predict the next word, which makes them good at generating text.

As these models grew bigger, they gained surprising abilities: following instructions, learning from a few examples in the prompt, and reasoning step by step~\cite{brown2020gpt3}. But they also hallucinate, meaning they sometimes produce answers that sound right but are factually wrong. In education this is a real problem because a student might trust a confident-sounding but incorrect explanation.

\subsubsection{Gemini}

Gemini is Google's multimodal model family~\cite{team2023gemini}. We use it for two things in this thesis. First, for digitization: its vision capabilities let us convert scanned math documents into \LaTeX{}. Second, for answer generation: it powers all three system variants (prompt-only, RAG, and hybrid). Using the same model for both tasks was mainly a practical choice that kept the implementation simpler on Vertex AI.

\subsection{Text Embeddings and Semantic Similarity}
\label{subsec:embeddings-background}

Text embeddings turn a piece of text into a vector of numbers. The idea is that texts with similar meaning end up close together in vector space. Early methods like Word2Vec worked at the word level, but Sentence-BERT~\cite{reimers2019sentence_bert} showed that BERT can be fine-tuned to produce useful sentence-level embeddings where cosine similarity actually reflects how related two texts are. This made embedding-based retrieval practical.

\subsubsection{BGE-M3}

For our retrieval pipeline we chose BGE-M3~\cite{chen2024bge_m3}. Three properties made it a good fit. First, it is multilingual and covers French and Arabic, both of which appear in Tunisian Bac materials. Second, it handles long inputs (up to 8192 tokens), so we can embed full exercise--correction pairs without cutting them off. Third, it is open-source and runs locally, which avoids API costs and keeps things reproducible. Although the model also supports sparse and ColBERT-style retrieval, we only use its dense embeddings.

\subsubsection{Challenges of Mathematical Embeddings}

Math formulas carry meaning through their structure---fractions, exponents, subscripts---and that structure gets lost when you flatten everything into a line of text. Some researchers have tried building embeddings specifically for math~\cite{mansouri2019tangent, pfahler2020math_embeddings}, but those approaches are hard to apply to a corpus like ours that mixes natural language with formulas.

Our workaround is simple: we convert all math into \LaTeX{} before embedding. For example, $\frac{a}{b}$ becomes \texttt{\textbackslash frac\{a\}\{b\}}, which gives a general-purpose model something consistent to work with. It is not perfect, but combined with metadata filtering (chapter, document type) it works well enough in practice.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{subsec:rag-background}

\subsubsection{Core Idea}

RAG, introduced by Lewis et al.~\cite{lewis2020rag}, tackles a basic limitation of language models: their knowledge is frozen at training time. A RAG system adds a retrieval step that pulls relevant documents from an external source before generating an answer. The standard pipeline works in three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Indexing}: split documents into chunks, compute embeddings, store them in a vector database.
    \item \textbf{Retrieval}: given a query, find the $k$ most similar chunks by comparing embeddings.
    \item \textbf{Generation}: feed the retrieved chunks together with the query to the language model.
\end{enumerate}

For our use case this is attractive because we want the model to answer based on real Tunisian Bac materials, not just its general knowledge.

\subsubsection{Retrieval Methods}

There are two main approaches. Sparse methods like BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} match based on shared words---fast and easy to understand, but limited when the query and the document use different wording. Dense methods like DPR~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr} encode everything into vectors and search by nearest-neighbour, which captures meaning better but depends on having a good embedding model. We use dense retrieval with BGE-M3, plus metadata filtering as an additional signal.

\subsubsection{Chunking Strategies}

How you split documents matters a lot. Chunks that are too small lose context; chunks that are too big dilute the useful information. Zhao et al.~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking} showed that respecting natural document boundaries beats arbitrary splitting, and Chen et al.~\cite{chen2024mog} found that mixing different chunk sizes helps for diverse collections.

We use adaptive chunking: 1500-character chunks for corrections (to keep individual exercises self-contained) and 3000-character chunks for course material (to keep theorems together with their explanations).

\subsubsection{Advanced RAG Architectures}

Since the original RAG paper, many extensions have appeared~\cite{gao2024rag_survey}. RETRO~\cite{borgeaud2022retro} integrates retrieval into pre-training, Atlas~\cite{izacard2022atlas} jointly trains the retriever and generator, and Self-RAG~\cite{asai2023self_rag} lets the model decide when retrieval is even needed. Our hybrid engine is much simpler: it just routes queries based on retrieval confidence, which we describe later.

\subsection{Prompt Engineering}
\label{subsec:prompt-engineering}

Prompt engineering is about designing the input you give to a language model so it produces the kind of output you want. Unlike fine-tuning, it does not change the model itself---everything happens at inference time, which makes it cheap and fast to iterate~\cite{white2023prompt_patterns}.

\subsubsection{Prompting Strategies}

The simplest approach is zero-shot prompting: just describe the task. Few-shot prompting adds a handful of examples before the actual question, and Brown et al.~\cite{brown2020gpt3} showed this can dramatically improve results without any training. For reasoning tasks, chain-of-thought prompting asks the model to show its work step by step. Kojima et al.~\cite{kojima2022zero_shot_cot} found that even adding ``Let's think step by step'' helps, and Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022chain_of_thought} formalized this into a proper technique. Instruction-tuned models like InstructGPT~\cite{ouyang2022instructgpt} take this further by being specifically trained to follow natural-language instructions.

\subsubsection{Prompting for Math}

Math prompting is trickier than general text generation. A good math answer needs correct notation, logical order, and the right level of detail~\cite{imani2023mathprompter}. In the Tunisian Bac context, this means formal French mathematical phrasing, explicit logical connectors, and staying within the syllabus. We design our prompts manually rather than using automatic methods like APE~\cite{zhou2023ape}, because the target style is very specific.

\subsubsection{Prompts in RAG Systems}

In a RAG system, the prompt has to do double duty: tell the model what to answer \emph{and} how to use the retrieved documents~\cite{gao2024rag_survey}. In our prompt-only baseline, the prompt carries the full burden of enforcing style. In the RAG and hybrid systems, it also has to organize the interaction between the question and the retrieved material.

\subsection{Hybrid AI Architectures}
\label{subsec:hybrid-background}

By hybrid we mean a system that does not use the same strategy for every query. It combines retrieval-based and prompt-based generation and picks between them depending on how good the retrieved evidence is.

\subsubsection{Hybrid Retrieval}

The term hybrid retrieval usually refers to mixing sparse and dense methods~\cite{lin2021hybrid_retrieval}. Lin et al.\ showed that combining both often beats either alone. Our system is not a classic sparse+dense setup---retrieval stays dense---but we do use structured metadata (chapter, document type) alongside semantic similarity, so retrieval is not purely embedding-based.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Generation}

Some systems go further and let the model decide whether retrieval is even worth using. Self-RAG~\cite{asai2023self_rag} has the model reflect on whether the retrieved passages are relevant, and Jiang et al.~\cite{jiang2023active_rag} explore routing queries away from retrieval entirely when it is unlikely to help. These are more complex than what we do, but they share the same intuition: retrieval helps when it finds good matches, and hurts when it does not.

\subsubsection{Confidence-Based Routing}

Our hybrid engine follows a simple version of this idea. It tries retrieval first, then checks how good the results are using distance scores. If the match is strong, it works like a normal RAG system. If the match is mediocre, it blends retrieved material with prompt-based guidance. If the match is poor, it falls back to prompt-only generation. This came from a practical observation during development: forcing weak retrievals into the prompt often made answers worse, not better. The routing is related to broader work on when LLMs should trust their own knowledge versus external sources~\cite{mallen2023trust_llm}.

\subsection{Vector Databases}
\label{subsec:vector-db-background}

Vector databases are built for similarity search over high-dimensional vectors. Unlike regular databases that do exact matching, they find nearest neighbours in embedding space. Options include FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss}, Pinecone, Weaviate, and ChromaDB~\cite{chromadb2024}.

We went with ChromaDB because it runs locally (no external service needed), supports metadata filtering (important since we filter by chapter and document type), has a clean Python API, and lets us plug in our own embedding model.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{OCR and Mathematical Document Processing}
\label{subsec:ocr-background}

Turning scanned math documents into machine-readable text is hard~\cite{zanibbi2016math_retrieval}. Standard OCR systems struggle with formulas because math is two-dimensional: fractions stack vertically, subscripts sit below, integral bounds span multiple lines. Specialized tools like Mathpix~\cite{mathpix2024} handle this better, and recently multimodal models have also shown good results~\cite{wang2023ocrbench, mahdavi2019icdar_math}.

We use Gemini's vision capabilities because our documents mix French text, math formulas, and sometimes handwritten annotations---handling all of that in one pass is simpler than chaining multiple specialized tools.

\subsection{AI in Mathematics Education}
\label{subsec:ai-education}

AI tools for math education have grown rapidly~\cite{kasneci2023chatgpt_education}. General chatbots (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude) can solve problems but are not tied to any particular curriculum. Math tools (Wolfram Alpha, Photomath) are precise but ignore pedagogical context. Curriculum-aware platforms like Khan Academy's Khanmigo try to ground AI in educational frameworks, but they target English-speaking students.

As far as we know, no existing work combines the Tunisian Bac math curriculum, French-language correction conventions, and a RAG system built on real exam materials. That is the gap this thesis addresses.

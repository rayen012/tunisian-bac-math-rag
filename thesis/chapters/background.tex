\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

This section covers the theoretical foundations of the system: large language
models, text embeddings, retrieval-augmented generation, vector databases,
mathematical OCR, and AI in education.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Large Language Models}
\label{subsec:llm-background}

\subsubsection{Transformer Architecture}

The Transformer, introduced by Vaswani et al.~\cite{vaswani2017attention},
is the foundation of modern LLMs. Unlike recurrent networks, Transformers
process input sequences in parallel using \textit{self-attention}, where each
token attends to all other tokens:

\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V
\label{eq:attention}
\end{equation}

where $Q$, $K$, $V$ are query, key, and value matrices, and $d_k$ is the
key dimension. Multi-head attention runs this computation in parallel with
different projections, allowing the model to capture different types of
relationships.

\subsubsection{From BERT to Generative Models}

Two paradigms emerged from the Transformer:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Encoder-only models} like BERT~\cite{devlin2019bert},
    trained with masked language modeling, are used for understanding tasks
    and---importantly for this work---computing embeddings for retrieval.

    \item \textbf{Decoder-only models} like
    GPT-3~\cite{brown2020gpt3}, LLaMA~\cite{touvron2023llama}, and
    Gemini~\cite{team2023gemini}, trained autoregressively, excel at
    generation. These are the backbone of modern chatbots and are used in
    this thesis for answer generation.
\end{itemize}

Scaling has led to emergent capabilities: in-context learning, chain-of-thought
reasoning, and instruction following~\cite{brown2020gpt3}. However, these
models are prone to \textit{hallucination}---generating plausible but
incorrect content---which is particularly dangerous in education where
students trust the system's reasoning.

\subsubsection{Gemini}

Google's Gemini~\cite{team2023gemini} is a multimodal LLM family capable of
processing text, images, audio, and video. We use Gemini for two purposes:
(1)~\textbf{document digitization}, leveraging its vision capabilities to
convert scanned math documents into \LaTeX{}, and (2)~\textbf{answer
generation} in all three system variants. Using the same model family for
both tasks simplifies deployment on Vertex AI.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Text Embeddings and Semantic Similarity}
\label{subsec:embeddings-background}

Text embeddings map variable-length text to fixed-dimensional vectors where
semantic similarity corresponds to geometric proximity.
Sentence-BERT~\cite{reimers2019sentence_bert} showed that fine-tuning BERT
with a siamese architecture produces embeddings where cosine similarity
reliably reflects semantic relatedness, making neural embeddings practical
for large-scale retrieval.

\subsubsection{BGE-M3}

We selected BGE-M3~\cite{chen2024bge_m3} for three key properties:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multilingual}: supports 100+ languages including French and
    Arabic---both present in Tunisian Bac materials.

    \item \textbf{Long context}: 8192-token maximum, enough to embed entire
    exercise--correction pairs without truncation.

    \item \textbf{Open-source}: runs locally without API calls, ensuring
    reproducibility and data sovereignty.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Challenges of Mathematical Embeddings}

Mathematical formulas encode meaning through spatial structure (fractions,
subscripts, exponents) that is lost in linear text. Prior work by
Mansouri et al.~\cite{mansouri2019tangent} and Pfahler et
al.~\cite{pfahler2020math_embeddings} has explored specialized math
embeddings, but these require custom architectures not applicable to our
mixed text-and-formula content.

We sidestep this by digitizing math into \LaTeX{} before embedding.
\LaTeX{} provides a standardized linear serialization
(e.g., \texttt{\textbackslash frac\{a\}\{b\}} for $\frac{a}{b}$) that
general-purpose models can process. While imperfect, the combination of
\LaTeX{} text with metadata filtering (chapter, exercise type) compensates
for limitations in pure embedding similarity.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}
\label{subsec:rag-background}

\subsubsection{Core Idea}

RAG was introduced by Lewis et al.~\cite{lewis2020rag} to address a
fundamental LLM limitation: parametric knowledge is frozen at training time.
RAG augments a generative model with a retrieval component that fetches
relevant documents from an external knowledge base at inference time.

The basic pipeline has three stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Indexing}: documents are chunked, embedded, and stored in a
    vector database.
    \item \textbf{Retrieval}: the $k$ most similar chunks are retrieved by
    comparing query and document embeddings.
    \item \textbf{Generation}: retrieved chunks are concatenated with the
    query and fed to the LLM.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Retrieval Methods}

\textbf{Sparse methods} like BM25~\cite{robertson2009bm25} use
term-frequency statistics---efficient and interpretable but limited to
lexical overlap. \textbf{Dense methods} like DPR~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr}
encode text into neural vectors and retrieve by nearest-neighbor search,
capturing semantic meaning at the cost of needing training data. We use
dense retrieval with BGE-M3, augmented by metadata filtering as a
structured signal.

\subsubsection{Chunking Strategies}

How documents are split affects retrieval quality. Zhao et
al.~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking} showed that structure-aware
chunking---respecting natural document boundaries---outperforms arbitrary
character-level splitting. Chen et al.~\cite{chen2024mog} showed that
mixing chunk granularities improves retrieval for heterogeneous collections.

We use \textit{adaptive chunking}: smaller chunks (1500 characters) for
corrections to preserve exercise boundaries, and larger chunks (3000
characters) for course material to keep theorems and proofs together.

\subsubsection{Advanced RAG Architectures}

Extensions to the original RAG include
RETRO~\cite{borgeaud2022retro} (retrieval during pre-training),
Atlas~\cite{izacard2022atlas} (joint retriever-generator training), and
Self-RAG (self-reflection on when to retrieve). Our hybrid engine
contributes a simpler variant: \textit{confidence-based routing} that
selects between RAG and prompt-only generation based on retrieval distance.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Vector Databases}
\label{subsec:vector-db-background}

Vector databases are optimized for similarity search over high-dimensional
vectors using approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) algorithms. Options include
FAISS~\cite{johnson2019faiss}, Pinecone, Weaviate, and
ChromaDB~\cite{chromadb2024}.

We chose ChromaDB for its local persistence (no external dependencies),
native metadata filtering (used extensively for chapter and document-type
queries), clean Python API, and support for custom embedding functions.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{OCR and Mathematical Document Processing}
\label{subsec:ocr-background}

Converting scanned math documents into machine-readable text is a
long-standing challenge~\cite{zanibbi2016math_retrieval}. Standard OCR
fails on mathematical notation because formulas are two-dimensional:
fractions stack vertically, subscripts require spatial interpretation,
and integral bounds span multiple lines.

Traditional approaches use specialized systems like
Mathpix~\cite{mathpix2024}. More recently, multimodal LLMs have shown
strong math OCR capabilities~\cite{wang2023ocrbench, mahdavi2019icdar_math}.
We use Gemini's vision capabilities because it handles mixed French text,
mathematical formulas, and handwritten annotations in a single pass---a
combination that would require multiple tools in a traditional pipeline.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{AI in Mathematics Education}
\label{subsec:ai-education}

AI-based math education has gained attention with capable
LLMs~\cite{kasneci2023chatgpt_education}. General-purpose chatbots
(ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude) can solve math problems but lack curriculum
alignment. Math-specific tools (Wolfram Alpha, Photomath) excel at
computation but ignore curriculum context. Curriculum-aligned systems (Khan
Academy's Khanmigo) attempt to ground AI in educational frameworks but
target English-speaking markets.

To our knowledge, no prior work addresses the combination of: (1)~the
Tunisian Bac mathematics curriculum, (2)~French-language correction style
conventions, and (3)~a RAG approach grounded in actual examination materials.
This thesis fills that gap.

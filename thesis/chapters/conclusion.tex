\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}

This thesis presented and evaluated an AI tutor for the Tunisian
Baccalaureate mathematics examination, with a particular focus on
retrieval-augmented generation. The system included the full pipeline from
document collection and digitization to indexing, retrieval, and answer
generation. Three variants were compared: a RAG system grounded in
retrieved documents, a prompt-only baseline without retrieval, and a hybrid
system that adapted its behavior according to retrieval quality.
The blind evaluation showed a result that differed from the initial
expectation. The prompt-only baseline achieved the highest overall score
(4.32/5), ahead of both the RAG system (3.22/5) and the hybrid system
(3.25/5). All three systems passed the out-of-scope guardrail tests. The
digitization pipeline also produced generally good OCR quality, with an
average CER of 5.4\% and \LaTeX{} math expression accuracy of 97.1\%.

The main conclusion is that retrieval was not automatically beneficial in
this setting. Because the base model already had strong mathematical
knowledge, and because the retrieval corpus remained relatively limited,
careful prompt design proved more effective than retrieval-based grounding.
More broadly, the results suggest that the usefulness of RAG in educational
systems depends not only on the retrieval architecture, but also on corpus
quality, corpus coverage, and the strength of the underlying model.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future-work}

Several directions could extend this work. The most important is corpus
expansion. A larger and denser collection of past exams, exercise series,
and corrections would make it more likely that retrieval finds genuinely
relevant material instead of only approximate matches.
A second direction is broader evaluation. Repeating the blind assessment
with multiple teachers would make it possible to examine inter-rater
agreement, while a larger question set would support a more stable
comparison across categories.
Another important step would be a student study. The current thesis
evaluates answer quality from the perspective of an expert teacher, but not
actual learning outcomes. Testing the system with Bac students would help
determine whether the highest-rated answers also lead to better
understanding and exam preparation.
On the technical side, it would be useful to explore improved retrieval.
This could include embedding fine-tuning on Tunisian Bac material, more
precise retrieval calibration, or alternative ranking strategies designed to
better distinguish between chapter-level similarity and true topical fit.
Finally, the tutor could be made more interactive. Instead of only
generating full corrections, a future version could guide the student
step by step, react to partial attempts, and provide hints before revealing
the final solution. This would move the system closer to a real tutoring
assistant rather than a correction generator.

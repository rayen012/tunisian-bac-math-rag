\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation System}
\label{sec:rag-system}

This section presents the design and implementation of the Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG) system, which constitutes the central technical contribution
of this thesis. Unlike the Prompt-Only baseline described in
Section~\ref{sec:prompt-only}, the RAG system grounds every answer in
retrieved documents drawn from a curated corpus of Tunisian Baccalaureate
mathematics material. The pipeline comprises six stages: data acquisition,
mathematical document digitization, database construction and indexing,
embedding model selection, two-stage retrieval, and answer generation. Each
stage is described below with the reasoning behind every design decision,
supported by relevant literature where available and explicitly marked as an
implementation-specific choice where no direct precedent exists.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Data Acquisition and Paired Exercise–Correction Structure}
\label{subsec:data-acquisition}

The foundation of any RAG system is the quality and structure of the knowledge
base from which documents are retrieved~\cite{lewis2020rag, gao2024rag_survey}.
In our case, the target domain is the Tunisian Baccalaureate mathematics
examination (Section Mathématiques), a national standardized test whose
correction style follows precise rhetorical and logical conventions that
students must reproduce in order to earn full marks. No publicly available,
machine-readable corpus of these materials existed prior to this work; the
primary sources are printed examination booklets, handwritten correction
sheets, and scanned textbook pages.

We manually scanned the raw mathematical material—official Baccalaureate
examinations with their corrections, classroom exercise series (\textit{séries}),
homework assignments (\textit{devoirs de synthèse}), and textbook chapters—from
physical documents. A deliberate and consequential design decision was made
during acquisition: \textbf{each exercise was scanned together with its
corresponding correction as a single logical unit}, rather than separating
exercise statements from their solutions into independent files. We refer to
this strategy as \textit{paired exercise–correction acquisition}.

This decision was motivated by three observations. First, in mathematical
pedagogy, the correction is not merely an answer but a complete reasoning
chain whose structure depends on the exact phrasing and constraints of the
exercise statement. Separating them would sever the logical dependency between
question and answer. Second, the retrieval system benefits from operating over
units that contain both the problem setup and the solution method: when a
student asks a question, the most useful retrieval target is a complete
worked example, not an orphaned solution missing its premise. Third, research
on chunking granularity for RAG systems has shown that preserving the logical
structure of documents during indexing significantly improves retrieval
relevance. Zhao~et~al.~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking} demonstrated that
structure-aware chunking—segmenting documents according to their inherent
element types rather than at arbitrary character boundaries—leads to measurably
better retrieval accuracy compared to paragraph-level chunking, a finding
echoed in the broader literature on adaptive chunk
granularity~\cite{chen2024mog}. While these studies address financial and
general-purpose documents rather than mathematical exercises specifically, the
underlying principle applies directly: the exercise–correction pair constitutes
a natural structural unit in mathematical pedagogy, and its preservation during
acquisition respects the document's inherent logical boundaries.

No prior work has studied paired exercise–correction acquisition for
mathematical RAG systems in the specific context of the Tunisian Baccalaureate.
This design decision is therefore presented as a domain-specific contribution
of this thesis, grounded in the broader principles of structure-aware document
processing for retrieval systems.

\subsection{Mathematical Document Digitization}
\label{subsec:digitization}

Once the raw material was scanned, the next challenge was to convert images and
PDF scans of mathematical content into machine-readable text suitable for
embedding and retrieval. This is a non-trivial problem: mathematical documents
contain two-dimensional spatial structures (fractions, subscripts, superscripts,
integrals, matrices) that cannot be captured by standard one-dimensional OCR
pipelines designed for linear text~\cite{wang2023ocrbench,
mahdavi2019icdar_math}. A faithful digitization must produce output that
preserves both the natural-language text and the mathematical expressions, ideally
in a format such as \LaTeX{} that encodes structural relationships explicitly.

\subsubsection{Tool Selection: Mathpix versus Gemini}

We initially considered \textbf{Mathpix}, a commercial service specifically
designed for mathematical OCR that converts images of equations into \LaTeX{}
with high accuracy~\cite{mathpix2024}. However, Mathpix operates on a
per-page pricing model, and the cost of processing several hundred scanned
pages made it impractical for a bachelor thesis prototype with limited
resources.

As an alternative, we adopted \textbf{Google Gemini} (accessed through the
Vertex AI API), a multimodal large language model capable of interpreting
images and generating structured text output. Recent benchmarking has shown
that large multimodal models such as Gemini and GPT-4V achieve competitive
performance on document understanding tasks, including mathematical expression
recognition, though they still face challenges with handwritten formulas and
degraded scan quality~\cite{wang2023ocrbench}. The OCRBench evaluation by
Wang~et~al. found that Gemini achieved the highest overall score across a
comprehensive battery of OCR tasks, including handwritten mathematical
expression recognition, among the multimodal models tested. For our use
case—primarily printed or cleanly handwritten Baccalaureate material—Gemini
provided sufficient fidelity at negligible marginal cost, since Vertex AI
usage was covered under an existing cloud project allocation.

It is important to note that no peer-reviewed study directly compares Gemini
and Mathpix for mathematical document digitization as of the time of writing.
The choice of Gemini over Mathpix was therefore an implementation constraint
driven by cost rather than a claim of superior accuracy. In a production
system with dedicated funding, a specialized tool like Mathpix might yield
higher fidelity for edge cases such as heavily degraded scans or dense matrix
notation.

\subsubsection{Digitization Pipeline (\texttt{digitize.py})}

The digitization pipeline, implemented in the \texttt{digitize.py} module,
operates as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Source enumeration.} The script scans a Google Cloud Storage
    (GCS) bucket under a designated prefix (\texttt{BacMath\_Raw\_Data/}) and
    identifies all image and PDF files with supported extensions
    (\texttt{.pdf}, \texttt{.png}, \texttt{.jpg}, \texttt{.jpeg},
    \texttt{.webp}).

    \item \textbf{Deduplication check.} For each source file, the script
    checks whether a companion \texttt{.tex} file already exists in the same
    GCS path (e.g., \texttt{image.png} $\to$ \texttt{image.png.tex}). Files
    with an existing companion are skipped, making the pipeline
    \textit{resume-friendly}: it can be interrupted and restarted without
    re-processing completed files.

    \item \textbf{Multimodal transcription.} Each pending file is sent to the
    Gemini model along with a structured prompt that instructs the model to
    act as a Tunisian mathematics teacher and transcribe the document
    faithfully into clean \LaTeX{}, preserving the official redaction
    style (``On a\ldots'', ``Donc\ldots'', ``D'après\ldots''), using proper
    mathematical environments (\texttt{align*}, \texttt{cases},
    \texttt{enumerate}), and marking illegible passages with
    \texttt{[ILLISIBLE]}. The prompt explicitly requests that exercises and
    corrections be separated with \texttt{\textbackslash section*\{Énoncé\}}
    and \texttt{\textbackslash section*\{Correction\}} headings.

    \item \textbf{Upload and logging.} The resulting \LaTeX{} text is uploaded
    back to GCS as the companion \texttt{.tex} file. Failures are logged to a
    JSONL report for later inspection and selective retry.
\end{enumerate}

The pipeline includes exponential-backoff retry logic (three attempts with
delays of 3, 6, and 12 seconds) to handle transient API errors, and supports
batch-size limiting (\texttt{-{}-max\_files}) for controlled processing runs.

\subsubsection{Bug Discovery and Fix}

During development, we discovered a subtle bug in an earlier version of the
deduplication logic: the script checked only for companion files of the form
\texttt{image.png.tex} but not \texttt{image.tex} (without the original
extension). This caused files that had already been digitized under a slightly
different naming convention to be re-processed, wasting API calls and
potentially creating duplicate entries. After identifying this issue through
inspection of the GCS bucket, we corrected the deduplication check to
consistently use the \texttt{<original\_filename>.tex} convention, ensuring
idempotent behavior. While minor in isolation, this kind of robustness issue
matters in a thesis prototype where API budgets are limited and reproducibility
of the corpus is important.

\subsection{Database Construction and Indexing}
\label{subsec:database-construction}

With the corpus digitized into \LaTeX{} files stored in GCS, the next stage is
to transform these files into an indexed, searchable vector database. This is
handled by the \texttt{build\_db.py} module, which performs normalization,
chunking, metadata extraction, embedding, and storage.

\subsubsection{From JSON Vector Store to ChromaDB}

In an early prototype of this system, we stored document embeddings in a simple
JSON-based vector store: each chunk's embedding vector, text, and metadata were
serialized into a JSON file, and similarity search was performed by loading all
vectors into memory and computing distances with NumPy. This approach was
functional for initial experiments but quickly revealed its limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scalability.} Loading the entire vector set into memory for
    every query is feasible for dozens of chunks but becomes impractical as
    the corpus grows to hundreds or thousands.
    \item \textbf{No incremental updates.} Adding new documents required
    regenerating the entire JSON file, re-embedding all chunks even if only
    one file changed.
    \item \textbf{No metadata filtering.} The JSON store had no native support
    for filtering by document type, chapter, or year during retrieval; all
    filtering had to be implemented ad hoc in application code.
    \item \textbf{No persistence guarantees.} The JSON file could be corrupted
    by concurrent writes or interrupted saves, with no transactional safety.
\end{itemize}

We migrated to \textbf{ChromaDB}, an open-source vector database designed for
embedding storage and similarity search in AI applications. ChromaDB provides
persistent local storage (backed by SQLite for metadata and an HNSW index for
vectors), native metadata filtering, deterministic upsert operations, and a
Python API that integrates directly with custom embedding
functions~\cite{chromadb2024}. For a thesis prototype operating on a
domain-specific corpus of moderate size (approximately 2{,}000 chunks),
ChromaDB offers the right balance between operational simplicity and the
features needed for a robust RAG pipeline. While managed cloud vector databases
such as Pinecone or Weaviate provide additional scalability features, they
introduce external dependencies and costs that are unnecessary at the scale of
this project.

The broader RAG literature consistently emphasizes the importance of
purpose-built vector storage for retrieval systems. Karpukhin~et~al.
demonstrated that dense passage retrieval with efficient vector indexing
(using FAISS in their case) dramatically outperforms sparse retrieval
methods like BM25 for open-domain question answering~\cite{karpukhin2020dpr}.
The architectural principle—precomputing dense embeddings and searching them
via approximate nearest neighbors—is the same whether the index is stored in
FAISS, ChromaDB, or any other vector database. Our choice of ChromaDB
reflects the practical constraint of needing a self-contained, locally
persistent store that supports metadata-filtered retrieval without requiring
a separate server process.

\subsubsection{Text Normalization}

Before chunking, each downloaded \texttt{.tex} file undergoes normalization:
line endings are standardized (CRLF $\to$ LF), consecutive whitespace is
collapsed, and runs of three or more newlines are reduced to double newlines.
This ensures consistent chunk boundaries regardless of the formatting
idiosyncrasies introduced during the Gemini transcription step.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Chunking Strategy}

Not all documents in the corpus have the same internal structure. Correction
documents (Baccalaureate exams, exercise series, homework) tend to be organized
around discrete exercises, each with clearly numbered parts. Textbook chapters,
by contrast, contain long-form theorem statements, proofs, and explanatory
text where meaning spans larger passages. A uniform chunk size would either
fragment corrections mid-exercise or produce excessively small textbook chunks
that lose theorem context.

We therefore implement \textit{adaptive chunking}: the chunk size and overlap
are determined by the document type. Correction-style documents (\texttt{bac\_officiel},
\texttt{serie}, \texttt{devoir}) use a smaller chunk size of 1{,}500
characters with 200 characters of overlap, designed to preserve individual
exercise boundaries. Textbook and course material (\texttt{cours},
\texttt{textbook}) use a larger chunk size of 3{,}000 characters with 250
characters of overlap, keeping theorem statements and their proofs within a
single chunk whenever possible. A default fallback of 1{,}800 characters with
200 characters of overlap is used for documents that do not match either
category.

The chunking algorithm itself prefers natural text boundaries over arbitrary
character positions. When a chunk reaches its target size, the algorithm
searches the last 40\% of the segment for the best available break point,
prioritizing in order: paragraph breaks (double newline), line breaks
(single newline), sentence endings (period followed by space), and finally
any whitespace. This boundary-aware splitting reduces the likelihood of
cutting through a mathematical derivation or a mid-sentence logical argument.

This approach aligns with recent findings by Zhao~et~al., who showed that
structure-aware chunking significantly outperforms naive fixed-size splitting
for RAG retrieval quality~\cite{zhao2024financial_chunking}. Chen~et~al.
further proposed Mix-of-Granularity, a framework that dynamically selects
optimal chunk granularity based on query characteristics, confirming that
no single chunk size is optimal for heterogeneous document
collections~\cite{chen2024mog}.

\subsubsection{Metadata Extraction}
\label{subsubsec:metadata}

Each chunk is annotated with a rich metadata schema extracted from the GCS
blob path and file naming conventions. The metadata fields are:

\begin{description}
    \item[\texttt{type}] Document category, inferred from path keywords:
    \texttt{bac\_officiel} (official Baccalaureate exams),
    \texttt{serie} (exercise series), \texttt{devoir} (homework),
    \texttt{cours} (textbook/course material), or \texttt{exercice} (generic).

    \item[\texttt{year}] Examination year, extracted via regex from patterns
    such as \texttt{Bac2019} or \texttt{bac\_2022}.

    \item[\texttt{session}] Examination session: \texttt{principal} (main
    session) or \texttt{controle} (resit session), parsed from path tokens.

    \item[\texttt{exo\_id}] Exercise number within the examination, extracted
    from patterns such as \texttt{Ex3} or \texttt{exercice\_2}.

    \item[\texttt{is\_solution}] Boolean flag (stored as the string
    \texttt{"true"} or \texttt{"false"} due to ChromaDB metadata constraints)
    indicating whether the chunk comes from a correction or an exercise
    statement. Detected by the presence of path indicators such as
    \texttt{\_sol}, \texttt{corrig}, or \texttt{correction}.

    \item[\texttt{chapter}] Mathematical topic, cleaned from the folder name
    (e.g., \texttt{10\_Nombres\_complexes} $\to$ \texttt{Nombres complexes}).

    \item[\texttt{group\_id}] The parent folder name, used to link exercise
    statements and their corrections that originate from the same source
    document. This field is critical for the companion retrieval mechanism
    described in Section~\ref{subsec:paired-retrieval}.

    \item[\texttt{filename}] Original file name for traceability.

    \item[\texttt{blob\_name}] Full GCS path.

    \item[\texttt{source}] GCS URI (\texttt{gs://bucket/path}) for citation
    in generated answers.
\end{description}

This metadata schema serves two purposes. At retrieval time, it enables
filtered search: the first retrieval pass queries only correction-type
documents with \texttt{is\_solution=true}, while the second pass targets
textbook material (see Section~\ref{subsec:retrieval-engine}). At the
companion-pairing stage, the combination of \texttt{chapter}, \texttt{type},
\texttt{year}, and \texttt{exo\_id} allows the system to locate the exercise
statement corresponding to a retrieved correction (see
Section~\ref{subsec:paired-retrieval}).

\subsubsection{Incremental Indexing and Manifest Tracking}

Re-embedding the entire corpus on every update would be prohibitively slow:
embedding a single chunk with BGE-M3 takes on the order of hundreds of
milliseconds, and the full corpus contains over a thousand chunks. To avoid
this, \texttt{build\_db.py} maintains a \textit{manifest} file
(\texttt{index\_manifest.json}) that tracks the state of every indexed file.

For each \texttt{.tex} blob in GCS, the manifest records:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The GCS \textit{generation} number (a version identifier that changes
    whenever the blob is modified).
    \item A SHA-256 hash of the normalized text content.
    \item The list of chunk IDs produced from that file.
    \item Key metadata fields (type, year, chapter, etc.) for statistics.
\end{itemize}

When the indexing script runs, it compares each blob's current generation
against the manifest. If the generation is unchanged, the file is skipped
entirely. If the generation has changed but the content hash is identical
(indicating a metadata-only update in GCS), the manifest is updated without
re-embedding. Only when the actual text content has changed does the system
delete the old chunks from ChromaDB and insert new ones. This two-level check
(generation, then content hash) reduces re-indexing time from the order of
minutes to seconds for unchanged corpora.

Chunk IDs follow a deterministic scheme:
\texttt{gs://bucket/path.tex::chunk\_0}, \texttt{::chunk\_1}, etc. This
ensures that upsert operations replace the exact same logical chunk rather
than creating duplicates, a property that is essential for maintaining index
consistency across incremental updates.

The manifest is saved atomically using a write-to-temporary-file followed by
\texttt{os.replace()}, which is atomic on POSIX systems, preventing
corruption from interrupted writes.

\subsection{Embedding Model Selection}
\label{subsec:embedding-selection}

The quality of a dense retrieval system is fundamentally bounded by the
discriminative power of its embedding model. If two semantically different
queries map to similar vectors, the retrieval stage cannot distinguish between
them, and no amount of prompt engineering or re-ranking can recover the
lost information. This constraint is especially acute in mathematical
domains, where notational differences that are visually and linguistically
minimal—such as $v(n)$ versus $u(n)$, or $\sin(x)$ versus $\cos(x)$—carry
entirely different semantic content.

\subsubsection{Initial Experience with Google Embedding Models}

Our first implementation used a Google-provided embedding model (accessed
through the Vertex AI Embeddings API). During testing, we observed that this
model did not reliably distinguish between queries involving closely related
mathematical sequences. For instance, a question about the convergence of a
sequence $v(n)$ would retrieve chunks discussing $u(n)$, and vice versa,
despite these being distinct sequences with different recursive definitions
and convergence behaviors in a given exercise. This is not surprising in
hindsight: general-purpose text embedding models are trained on natural
language corpora where single-character differences rarely change the overall
meaning of a passage. In mathematics, however, such differences are
semantically decisive.

This observation is consistent with the broader literature on the limitations
of text embeddings for mathematical content. Research on mathematical
information retrieval has established that mathematical expressions have
inherently two-dimensional spatial structures and can be written in multiple
semantically equivalent forms, making them fundamentally different from linear
natural-language text~\cite{mansouri2019tangent, zanibbi2016math_retrieval}.
Standard text embedding models, which are optimized for natural-language
similarity, tend to treat mathematical symbols as low-frequency tokens with
weak learned representations, leading to poor discrimination between
structurally similar but semantically distinct
expressions~\cite{pfahler2020math_embeddings}.

It is important to note that this observation—poor discrimination between
$v(n)$ and $u(n)$—is an empirical finding from our own testing, not a
controlled experiment with published metrics. We did not conduct a systematic
ablation study comparing embedding models on mathematical retrieval benchmarks.
The decision to switch models was driven by qualitative observation of
retrieval failures during iterative development.

\subsubsection{Migration to BGE-M3}

We replaced the Google embedding model with \textbf{BGE-M3}
(\textit{Multi-Linguality, Multi-Functionality, Multi-Granularity}), developed
by the Beijing Academy of Artificial Intelligence
(BAAI)~\cite{chen2024bgem3}. BGE-M3 is built on an XLM-RoBERTa backbone,
continually pre-trained with RetroMAE, and supports input sequences of up to
8{,}192 tokens. It achieves state-of-the-art results on multilingual
(MIRACL), cross-lingual (MKQA), and long-document retrieval benchmarks.

Several characteristics of BGE-M3 make it particularly suitable for our use
case:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Multilingual support.} The Tunisian Baccalaureate
    mathematics corpus is written in French with embedded \LaTeX{} mathematical
    notation and occasional Tunisian Arabic (Derja) terms. BGE-M3 supports
    over 100 languages, including French and Arabic, and was trained on
    multilingual retrieval tasks, making it robust to the code-switching
    present in our documents.

    \item \textbf{Long context window.} With a maximum input length of 8{,}192
    tokens, BGE-M3 can embed our larger textbook chunks (up to 3{,}000
    characters) without truncation, preserving the full semantic content of
    theorem-length passages.

    \item \textbf{Multi-granularity training.} The model was trained on inputs
    ranging from short sentences to long documents, which aligns with the
    heterogeneous nature of our corpus (short exercise statements alongside
    long course sections).

    \item \textbf{Dense retrieval quality.} BGE-M3 simultaneously supports
    dense, sparse, and multi-vector retrieval. We use only the dense
    retrieval mode (extracting the \texttt{dense\_vecs} output), which already
    provides superior discrimination on our mathematical content compared to
    the previous model—an observation we confirmed qualitatively by verifying
    that the $v(n)$/$u(n)$ confusion no longer occurred in retrieval results.
\end{enumerate}

The embedding function is implemented as a custom ChromaDB
\texttt{EmbeddingFunction} class that wraps the \texttt{BGEM3FlagModel} from
the FlagEmbedding library. The model is loaded once as a singleton and reused
across all embedding calls, with automatic GPU detection (\texttt{fp16=True}
when CUDA is available, \texttt{fp16=False} on CPU) for hardware-adaptive
performance.

\subsection{RAG Retrieval and Generation Engine}
\label{subsec:retrieval-engine}

The core runtime pipeline is implemented in the \texttt{rag\_engine.py} module,
which encapsulates the complete retrieve–compile–generate cycle in a single
class (\texttt{TunisianMathRAG}). This module is deliberately
\textit{UI-agnostic}: it exposes a \texttt{.query(question, mode)} method
that returns a structured \texttt{QueryResult} dataclass, and can be called
from a Streamlit interface, a Jupyter notebook, or a command-line test script
without modification. This separation of concerns was essential for thesis
evaluation, where we needed to run the same engine across different testing
interfaces while ensuring consistent behavior.

\subsubsection{Two-Stage Retrieval}
\label{subsubsec:two-stage}

The retrieval strategy is the most distinctive architectural feature of the
RAG engine. Rather than performing a single undifferentiated similarity search
over the entire corpus, the engine executes a \textit{two-stage retrieval}
pipeline that prioritizes correction-style documents before falling back to
textbook material.

\paragraph{First pass: correction exemplars.}
The first retrieval pass searches exclusively among correction-type documents
(\texttt{type} $\in$ \{\texttt{bac\_officiel}, \texttt{serie},
\texttt{devoir}\} with \texttt{is\_solution} = \texttt{true}), retrieving up
to $k_1 = 10$ candidates. The rationale is pedagogical: when a student asks a
question, the most directly useful reference is a previously corrected exercise
that is similar in structure and topic. Such a correction provides not just the
mathematical content of the answer, but also the \textit{rhetorical model}—the
step-by-step redaction style, the logical connectors (``On a\ldots'',
``Or\ldots'', ``Donc\ldots''), and the level of detail expected by
Baccalaureate examiners. By presenting these correction exemplars in the LLM's
context window, we enable a form of \textit{style mimicry}: the model is
instructed to reproduce the method and redaction style of the retrieved
correction, adapting it to the student's specific question.

\paragraph{Second pass: textbook fallback.}
If the best distance from the first pass exceeds a threshold (see below),
indicating that no sufficiently similar correction exists in the corpus, the
engine executes a second retrieval pass targeting textbook and course material
(\texttt{type} $\in$ \{\texttt{cours}, \texttt{textbook}\}), retrieving up to
$k_2 = 8$ candidates. These documents provide the theoretical backing—theorem
statements, properties, and definitions—needed to construct a solution from
first principles. In this case, the system prompt instructs the LLM to build
the answer using the retrieved theorems while maintaining the Baccalaureate
redaction conventions.

If both passes fail to produce relevant results, the engine falls back to an
unfiltered search over the entire corpus as a last resort, applying a relaxed
distance threshold.

\paragraph{Pedagogical motivation.}
This two-stage strategy reflects a deliberate pedagogical design. In the
Tunisian Baccalaureate context, the distinction between ``solving an
exercise'' and ``knowing the theory'' is fundamental. A correction exemplar
teaches the student \textit{how to write} a solution in the expected format;
a textbook reference teaches \textit{what mathematical tools to use}. By
separating these two retrieval passes, the system can compose its context
from the most appropriate source for each query, rather than mixing
corrections and theory indiscriminately.

\subsubsection{Similarity Thresholds}

The decision between the two retrieval stages is governed by two distance
thresholds applied to the L2 distances returned by ChromaDB's HNSW index
(lower distance = higher similarity):

\begin{description}
    \item[\texttt{SIMILARITY\_GOOD\_THRESHOLD} = 1.2]
    If the best distance from the first pass is at or below this value, the
    system considers the correction match to be strong (Case~A) and uses only
    the correction-pass results, supplemented by companion exercise statements.

    \item[\texttt{SIMILARITY\_FALLBACK\_THRESHOLD} = 1.6]
    Documents with distances above this value are discarded entirely, regardless
    of which retrieval pass produced them. This prevents the injection of
    semantically irrelevant content into the LLM's context, which could
    degrade answer quality through noise.
\end{description}

These thresholds were determined empirically through iterative testing during
development. We observed the distribution of L2 distances returned by BGE-M3
embeddings on a variety of test queries and noted that distances below
approximately 1.2 consistently corresponded to retrieved corrections that were
genuinely relevant to the query, while distances above approximately 1.6
produced corrections on unrelated topics. The intermediate range (1.2--1.6)
corresponded to partially relevant results that might share a chapter or
exercise type but not the specific mathematical content of the query.

We make no claim that these thresholds are theoretically optimal or
generalizable to other embedding models or corpora. They are
implementation-specific parameters calibrated to the behavior of BGE-M3 on
our particular Tunisian Baccalaureate corpus, and would likely require
recalibration if the embedding model, corpus size, or document domain were
changed.

\subsubsection{Context Compilation}

The selected documents (from one or both retrieval passes, plus any companion
exercise statements) are formatted into XML-tagged context blocks for
inclusion in the LLM prompt. Each block contains the document's GCS URI,
metadata labels (\texttt{type}, \texttt{chapter}, \texttt{year}), a role tag
(\texttt{[ÉNONCÉ]} for exercise statements, \texttt{[CORRECTION]} for
solutions), and the document content truncated to a maximum of 2{,}000
characters. The total context is capped at 14{,}000 characters to stay within
the model's effective context window while leaving room for the system prompt
and the student's question.

\subsubsection{Prompt Construction and Generation}

The system prompt defines the persona of ``Monsieur Tounsi'', a veteran
Tunisian mathematics teacher, and encodes several constraints:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Anti-hallucination rules:} The model must use \textit{only}
    information present in the retrieved context. If the context is
    insufficient, it must say so explicitly and provide only a method outline.
    \item \textbf{Mimicry decision tree:} In Case~A (correction found), the
    model is instructed to copy the method and redaction style of the retrieved
    correction. In Case~B (textbook only), it must construct the solution from
    retrieved theorems.
    \item \textbf{Syllabus guard:} Methods that are outside the Tunisian
    Baccalaureate curriculum (L'Hôpital's rule, Taylor series, matrix
    diagonalization, etc.) are explicitly forbidden.
    \item \textbf{Derja sandwich:} The answer begins and ends with
    motivational phrases in Tunisian Arabic dialect, with the mathematical
    body in formal academic French—a pedagogical convention familiar to
    Tunisian students.
    \item \textbf{Anti-injection rules:} The model is warned that the context
    may contain adversarial text and must never follow instructions embedded
    within retrieved documents.
\end{itemize}

The user prompt includes the interaction mode (\textit{correction} for a dry,
exam-style answer or \textit{coaching} for a pedagogical explanation), the
context blocks, the student's question, and final instructions requiring
explicit source citation.

Generation is performed via the Vertex AI Gemini API with a temperature of
0.15 (low but non-zero, allowing slight variation while favoring deterministic
mathematical reasoning) and a maximum output of 4{,}096 tokens. The generation
call includes exponential-backoff retry logic (up to 3 attempts with delays of
2, 4, and 8 seconds) to handle transient API failures.

\subsubsection{Confidence Levels and Observability}

Every query returns a \texttt{QueryResult} dataclass that captures not only
the generated answer but also full observability data: the list of documents
from each retrieval pass, their distances and metadata, the retrieval case
(A or B), timing breakdowns (retrieval time, generation time, total time),
and a confidence level.

The confidence level is computed from retrieval quality:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fort} (high): the best distance is $\leq 1.2$ and the
    total context exceeds 5{,}000 characters, indicating a strong match with
    substantial supporting material.
    \item \textbf{Moyen} (medium): the best distance is $\leq 1.6$ but
    either the match is not as close or the context is sparse.
    \item \textbf{Faible} (low): no documents were retrieved, or all
    distances exceed the fallback threshold.
\end{itemize}

This observability design was essential for thesis evaluation: it allowed us
to inspect every retrieval decision, verify that the correct documents were
being selected, and diagnose failure modes systematically.

\subsection{Paired Retrieval of Exercise and Correction}
\label{subsec:paired-retrieval}

During testing of the RAG engine, we identified a structural problem in the
retrieved context: the system would sometimes retrieve a correction chunk
without the corresponding exercise statement, or retrieve an exercise
statement without its correction. This occurs because the chunking process
may split an exercise–correction pair across chunk boundaries, and the
similarity search retrieves individual chunks independently of their
structural relationships.

This is problematic for mathematical reasoning. A correction is not
self-contained; it is a response to a specific exercise statement. The
notation, variable names, and given conditions in the exercise define the
scope of the correction. Without seeing the exercise statement, neither the
LLM nor a human reader can fully understand what the correction is solving.
Conversely, an exercise statement without its correction provides the problem
but not the solution method that the student needs.

\subsubsection{Companion Fetch Mechanism}

To address this, we implemented a \textit{companion fetch} mechanism in the
retrieval pipeline. After the two-stage retrieval selects its final set of
correction documents, the engine performs a metadata-based lookup to find the
corresponding exercise statement for each selected correction:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item For each correction chunk in the selected results, the engine
    extracts the key metadata fields: \texttt{chapter}, \texttt{type},
    \texttt{year}, and \texttt{exo\_id}.

    \item Using these fields, it constructs a metadata filter requesting
    chunks with the same values but \texttt{is\_solution} = \texttt{false}
    (i.e., exercise statements rather than corrections).

    \item This filter is executed via ChromaDB's \texttt{collection.get()}
    method, which performs a direct SQLite metadata lookup rather than a
    vector similarity search. This is both fast (single-digit milliseconds)
    and immune to the HNSW index synchronization issues that can affect
    filtered similarity queries.

    \item The retrieved exercise chunks (up to 3 per correction group) are
    prepended to the context, appearing before their corresponding
    corrections.
\end{enumerate}

In the context compilation step, companion exercise chunks are labeled with
\texttt{[ÉNONCÉ]} and assigned a special distance marker
(\texttt{companion (énoncé)}) to distinguish them from chunks retrieved via
similarity search. This allows the LLM to understand that these exercise
statements were included for context completeness rather than because they
matched the query directly.

\subsubsection{Significance for Mathematical RAG}

This companion fetch mechanism is, to our knowledge, not described in the
existing RAG literature, which typically assumes that each retrieved chunk is
independently useful. In general-purpose question-answering RAG
systems~\cite{lewis2020rag, karpukhin2020dpr}, this assumption is reasonable:
a retrieved Wikipedia paragraph can usually be understood in isolation.
However, in structured pedagogical documents where exercises and corrections
form logical pairs, this assumption breaks down.

The closest related concept in the literature is the notion of
\textit{parent-document retrieval}, where a retrieval system indexes smaller
chunks for precision but returns the larger parent document for
context~\cite{gao2024rag_survey}. Our companion fetch mechanism can be seen
as a specialized form of this pattern, adapted to the paired structure of
mathematical exercises: rather than returning a generic parent document, we
return the specific structural counterpart (exercise $\leftrightarrow$
correction) identified through metadata matching.

This design decision is presented as an implementation-specific contribution
to domain-adapted RAG for mathematics education. No prior work has, to our
knowledge, implemented exercise–correction companion retrieval in a
mathematical tutoring system.

\subsection{Connection to the Tunisian Baccalaureate Context}
\label{subsec:bac-context}

The design decisions described in this section are not arbitrary architectural
choices; they are motivated by the specific requirements of generating
Tunisian Baccalaureate-style mathematical solutions. Four characteristics of
this domain shaped the system:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Curriculum boundaries.} The Tunisian Baccalaureate
    (Section Mathématiques) covers a fixed set of ten chapters, and examiners
    penalize solutions that use methods outside the official program. The
    system must therefore respect these boundaries, which is enforced through
    both the syllabus guard in the prompt and the metadata filtering that
    restricts retrieval to curriculum-aligned material.

    \item \textbf{Formal redaction conventions.} Baccalaureate corrections
    follow a strict rhetorical style: numbered steps, logical connectors in
    formal French (``On a\ldots'', ``D'après le théorème de\ldots'',
    ``Donc\ldots''), and concluding statements. The two-stage retrieval
    strategy prioritizes correction exemplars precisely so that the LLM can
    mimic this redaction style, producing answers that look and read like
    official corrections rather than generic mathematical explanations.

    \item \textbf{Exercise–correction dependency.} In Tunisian mathematical
    pedagogy, corrections are always read alongside their exercises.
    The companion fetch mechanism preserves this paired structure in the
    retrieved context, ensuring that the LLM has access to the full logical
    unit it needs to produce a coherent answer.

    \item \textbf{Bilingual and dialectal context.} The corpus is primarily
    in French with \LaTeX{} mathematical notation, but student questions may
    include Tunisian Arabic (Derja) terms, and the pedagogical convention
    expects motivational phrases in Derja. The choice of BGE-M3, with its
    multilingual training on over 100 languages including French and Arabic,
    supports this bilingual operating context.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these design choices produce a RAG system that is not a generic
question-answering pipeline adapted to mathematics, but a purpose-built tool
for a specific educational domain with specific stylistic, curricular, and
linguistic requirements.
